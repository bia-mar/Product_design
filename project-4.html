Revolut — The Shared Pouch

Product Design • 3 designers • Academic project • iOS — Figma

Context

A joint account without the commitment of a joint account. The Shared Pouch is a concept feature for Revolut that lets any group of people — friends, couples, colleagues — open a shared money pool, contribute in any currency, and spend from it directly. No one needs to front the money. No one needs to ask for approval. Everyone can see every transaction in real time.

The idea

A shared account for life's in-between moments

Traditional joint accounts are a big commitment — full bank onboarding for everyone, designed for long-term financial relationships. But most shared money situations aren't long-term. They're a group trip. A couple moving in together for the first time. Friends splitting a renovation.

The Shared Pouch sits between "split it after" and "open a joint account." Everyone contributes to a common pot, gets a virtual card, and spends directly — no approval needed, no one fronting cash. Multiple currencies feed the same balance. When the shared moment ends, any remaining money is automatically returned to each contributor.

Group travel

Friends pool money for shared expenses. Everyone spends, nobody keeps score in real time.

Moving in together

A couple creates a Pouch for shared flat costs — furniture, deposits, utilities — without opening a formal joint account.

Shared project

Colleagues or flatmates pool for a specific goal. Spend when needed, settle automatically when done.

Revolut already handles individual multi-currency spending better than almost anyone. But the moment users need to share money with a group, they leave the app. The tools they reach for are workarounds — none of them move money, none of them give everyone a card. The Shared Pouch proposes that Revolut owns this space natively, rather than letting 20 million users manage it elsewhere.

Problem

Shared money is full of friction — before, during, and after

Current pain points

  • Someone always fronts the money
  • Tracking across currencies is manual
  • Settlement is delayed and awkward
  • Non-Revolut users are excluded
  • No tool owns the full loop: pool, spend, settle

What the Pouch solves

  • Communal pool — no one fronts anything
  • Spend directly from the shared account
  • Guests join without a full Revolut account
  • Multi-currency contributions to one pot
  • Automated settlement when the Pouch closes

Process

From insight to feature architecture

Benchmarking covered both the Revolut app and the tools currently filling this gap. The goal was to understand not just what was missing, but why existing solutions weren't enough — Splitwise tracks debt but doesn't move money, Wise moves money but isn't built for groups, HyperJar pools money but lacks flexibility for international use.

Research mapped the customer journey across different use cases and identified where the friction was highest. The key finding: the problem isn't settling up at the end — it's the moment of spending, when someone has to decide who pays, who owes what, and how to coordinate in real time.

AI in the process — Figma Make

Before committing to any design direction, the team used AI — specifically Figma Make — to rapidly visualize the core concept. A structured prompt defined the feature architecture, the user flows, and Revolut's design language. The AI generated initial mockups that made the idea tangible without spending time in Figma. This wasn't about generating final design — it was about pressure-testing the concept visually, fast. Think of it as a sketchpad that speaks Figma.

The actual design work came after. To ensure the feature felt native to Revolut, I studied the app directly — creating a group chat, exploring the existing shared expenses UI, and mapping the patterns Revolut already uses. The final mockups were built in Figma from scratch, mixing the visual language of Revolut's personal account with the group patterns already in the product. The goal: a feature that a Revolut user would open and immediately feel at home in.

Design decision — the Anchor model

The biggest constraint was regulatory: you can't open a joint bank account for a group without full KYC for each person. The solution was decoupling legal ownership from spending rights. One fully verified Revolut user — the Anchor — owns the pot legally. Guests get temporary spending rights via virtual cards without full bank onboarding. A Shadow Ledger tracks each person's contribution in real time, ensuring accountability without requiring everyone to be a full Revolut customer.

Design decision — UI language

The Pouch deliberately mirrors Revolut's personal account layout — the same balance display, transaction feed, and quick-action bar. The goal was for the feature to feel like a natural extension of the app, not a separate product. When something new looks like something familiar, the learning curve disappears.

My contribution

Feature mapping, flow design, and UI

My role covered three areas: the initial feature mapping and flow architecture — translating the concept into a structured user journey; the benchmarking of Revolut's existing app to identify where the Pouch should live and how it should behave; and the UI design for the core screens outside the main dashboard — transaction detail, split editing, fund request, and the guest onboarding flow.

Business case

What's in it for Revolut

The Shared Pouch is a growth mechanic disguised as a feature. Every time an Anchor creates a Pouch and invites guests, Revolut gets direct exposure to non-users. When those guests experience the product first-hand — contributing money, getting a virtual card, seeing their balance in real time — the conversion opportunity is built into the interaction. The feature turns every shared moment into a low-friction acquisition funnel.

Revolut's stated goal is 100 million daily active users. The Shared Pouch makes existing users the distribution channel — every Pouch created is an invitation to join.

Limitations & next steps

Honest constraints

This is a concept feature, not a validated product. The regulatory architecture was designed with compliance intent but not validated with legal or engineering teams. Real implementation would require significant regulatory work across Revolut's operating markets.

The next iteration would focus on partial contributors, an emergency refill mechanic for when the pot runs dry, and a conversion incentive that turns guest users into full Revolut accounts when the Pouch closes.
